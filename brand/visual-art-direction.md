# Neckarstücke — Visual Art Direction
## First Collection: Principles & Reference Framework

> Version 1.0 · April 2026  
> Status: Foundation document — governs all motif production for the first collection  
> Scope: Illustration style, composition, colour philosophy, typography-in-motif, quality criteria

---

## 1. Curated Reference Set

The following works define the visual DNA of the first collection. Each entry notes the specific quality to absorb — not to copy, but to internalise.

---

### 1.1 WPA / National Park Service Posters (1930s–1940s)

**Zion National Park — WPA/NPS, c. 1938**  
[Style: bold flat planes of colour, extreme vertical framing of canyon walls]  
*Take from this:* The willingness to cut the frame and let geology dominate. The sky is a single flat colour — no gradients, no clouds. The scale of the landscape is communicated through contrast, not detail.

**Grand Canyon National Park — WPA/NPS, c. 1938**  
[Style: layered colour planes suggesting depth without perspective tricks]  
*Take from this:* How successive receding planes (foreground ridge → mid valley → distant wall → sky) build space through tonal darkening, not aerial perspective. Each plane is its own colour — no blending at the edge.

**Olympic National Park — WPA/NPS, c. 1938**  
[Style: silhouetted foreground trees against lit mid-ground, sky reading as solid colour field]  
*Take from this:* The silhouette as the primary compositional device. When the foreground element is dark and graphic, the middle ground carries the colour. The viewer's eye moves across planes, not into them.

**Shenandoah National Park — WPA/NPS, c. 1938**  
[Style: rolling hills in a restricted 4-colour palette, harvest-season warmth]  
*Take from this:* Seasonal and landscape-specific colour palettes — a late-summer palette is different from an autumn one and both are different from early spring. The colour is the time of year as much as it is the place.

---

### 1.2 NASA Exoplanet Travel Posters — Visions of the Future Series (2016)

**"Visit Trappist-1e" — NASA/JPL, 2016**  
[Style: pure Art Deco silhouette, two-colour plus highlight, total compositional geometry]  
*Take from this:* Restraint as statement. When you have only two colours plus white, every element must justify its presence. Negative space becomes an active element.

**"The Grand Tour" — NASA/JPL, 2016**  
[Style: mid-century travel poster, bold typographic integration, atmospheric gradient restricted to sky zone only]  
*Take from this:* The only place a gradient is permitted is sky. Everything else is flat. This is the rule.

**"Kepler-186f" — NASA/JPL, 2016**  
[Style: silhouetted double figures against a warm atmospheric background, extreme foreground/background contrast]  
*Take from this:* The human (or non-human) figure as scale anchor. If the landscape dwarfs the figure, the scale of the place is felt immediately. The figure is never the subject — the place is.

---

### 1.3 German Regional & Transport Posters (1920s–1940s)

**Neckartal Railway Poster (generic era reference, Deutsche Reichsbahn, c. 1930)**  
[Style: elevated viewpoint, river in the valley, warm terracotta and cream tones, roman sans-serif typography]  
*Take from this:* The Neckar valley has its own poster tradition — the elevated viewpoint looking down into the valley, the river as a threading element, the warm limestone and terracotta palette. This is what the audience grew up seeing. Reference it, but exceed it technically.

**Heidelberg — Verkehrsverein Heidelberg, c. 1935**  
[Style: castle above city, hard geometric shadows, letterpress register]  
*Take from this:* Architectural subjects benefit from simplified geometry over topographic accuracy. The castle's silhouette is more recognisable reduced to planes than represented in photographic detail. Simplification is not loss — it is essence.

## 2. Illustration Approach

### 2.1 Medium

**Digital vector illustration, rendered in a restricted painterly mode.**

- Not photographic retouching, not hand-drawn scan. The production tool is Illustrator or an equivalent vector environment, but the *appearance* must reference hand-cut gouache or silkscreen printing — flat shapes with intentional edge quality.
- No photo-based textures, no lens effects, no depth-of-field blur.
- Grain or paper texture may be applied at the reproduction stage as a subtle print simulation — it is not part of the illustration itself.

### 2.2 Level of Abstraction

**High abstraction. Identify the essential; remove the rest.**

The goal is the essence of a place, not its documentation. A motif for Hirschhorn is not a view of Hirschhorn — it is the *feeling* of standing above it.

**Concretely:**
- A hillside is 2–4 tonal planes. Not individual trees.
- The river is a curved shape with a consistent value — not a reflection of the sky.
- Architecture is reduced to its geometric silhouette. Windows are gestural marks, not counted.
- Human figures (if present) are silhouettes — no faces, no identity, only scale and presence.

The test: can you print this at 5cm × 7cm as a thumbnail and still immediately understand the place? If yes, the abstraction is right.

### 2.3 Line Character

**Closed shapes, not open lines.**

These are not pen-and-ink illustrations. The primary vocabulary is shape, not stroke.

- Contour lines, if present, are part of the fill shape, not an outline on top of it.
- Hatching and cross-hatching are not part of this collection's visual language.
- Hard edges are preferred over soft transitions.
- An edge between two shapes may have a hairline gap (negative space at the seam) — this is a silkscreen registration reference and is permitted and desirable.

### 2.4 Light and Shadow

**Light source: high, consistent, and implied.**

WPA posters don't show shadows realistically — they show *which side of a shape is in light and which is in shade* as a colour plane decision.

**Rules:**
- Lit surfaces: lighter value of the motif's colour.
- Shadow: darker value, or the dark anchor colour (Neckar-Charcoal or its per-motif equivalent).
- No gradients within a shadow plane. A shadow is a shape, not a fade.
- Cast shadows may be suggested as a separate dark shape at the base of an object.
- The sky is always the lightest plane and always flat.

## 3. Compositional Principles

### 3.1 Framing

**Portrait orientation, approximately 2:3 ratio (A3 proportions).** The print format is vertical because:
- It is the natural orientation of walls and hanging space.
- Vertical framing forces the landscape to assert height — valley walls, castle keeps, hillsides — rather than horizontal sprawl.
- It matches the WPA poster canon.

**The frame does not contain the landscape — the landscape fills and sometimes exceeds the frame.** Elements may be cropped. The sky may not extend to the top edge if the subject demands it. Bleed is intentional.

### 3.2 Hierarchy

**One dominant element. One or two supporting elements. One background field.**

This is the composition rule. Not a suggestion.

- The dominant element occupies ≥ 40% of the picture plane.
- It sits within the middle half of the frame — not centred (centred is static), but anchored left or right of centre with deliberate offset.
- Supporting elements create context, not competition.
- The background field (sky or distant landscape) is the largest single colour field. It unites the composition.

**Avoid:** compositions where no single element dominates; compositions where detail competes equally across the full frame; symmetric arrangements.

### 3.3 Use of Negative Space

**Negative space is designed, not leftover.**

The sky, the river, open plateau — these are not empty areas waiting to be filled. They are compositional masses.

- Sky fields should be a deliberate shape — consider how the skyline of the land below creates the lower edge of the sky shape.
- Negative space should carry its colour with confidence. A large area of Valley Sage is a statement, not a void.
- Text (location name) placed within negative space anchors it — see Section 4.

### 3.4 Colour Fields Vs. the Subject

**The subject is read first. Colour fields reinforce, not compete.**

Each motif uses 4–6 colours total, drawn from the brand palette and one or two location-specific accent colours (decided at production time). The allocation:

| Role | Allocation |
|------|-----------|
| Sky / dominant background | 1 colour, lightest value |
| Land / middle ground | 1–2 colours, mid values |
| Subject (building, water feature, dominant form) | 1 colour, strongest contrast with background |
| Shadow / depth | 1 colour, darkest value — Neckar-Charcoal or near-equivalent |
| Accent (optional) | 1 colour, warm or cool counter to dominant hue — Ochre or a location-specific choice |

The background field is always the largest area. The subject is the highest-contrast element. Accent colour is ≤ 15% of the picture plane.

## 4. Typography-in-Motif Rule

### 4.1 The Rule

**The location name appears within the motif artwork as an integrated typographic element, not as a label applied on top.**

The text is part of the composition. It is laid in before the colour fields are finalised, and the composition responds to it.

### 4.2 Placement

- **Lower third** of the composition, flush left or flush right — aligned to the dominant element's axis.
- Alternatively: lower quarter, centred, if the composition is symmetrically arranged.
- Never centred-middle, never floating in the middle third.
- Minimum margin from any edge: 8% of the print short dimension.

### 4.3 Typographic Treatment

- **Typeface:** EB Garamond Regular, uppercase, tracked +0.12em — matching the wordmark register.
- **Size:** the location name occupies approximately 8–12% of the print width when set in full. Adjust to fit the compositional space.
- **Colour:** Paper (`#FAFAF5`) when placed over a dark colour field; Neckar-Charcoal (`#1C2826`) when placed over a light field. Valley Sage (`#3D6154`) is permitted as an alternative over Paper, if the composition calls for it.
- **The text does not have a bounding box, rule, or background block.** It sits in the colour field directly.
- A hairline rule above the location name (same colour, same width as the text block) is permitted as a compositional anchor — this references the logo wordmark structure.

### 4.4 What Does Not Appear in the Motif

- No brand name (NECKARSTÜCKE) within the motif. The brand appears only in the frame/packaging context.
- No edition number, no year, no web address.
- No descriptive subtext ("Blick vom Dilsberg" etc.) — the location name alone is sufficient.
- No tagline.

## 5. Museum-Quality Checklist

A motif is not ready for production until it passes all of the following. This is a gate, not a guideline.

---

**1. The essential is present, the incidental is absent.**  
Can you describe what the motif depicts in one noun phrase? If yes: proceed. If you need a clause to explain it: simplify further.

**2. The colour count is controlled.**  
Count the distinct colours in the composition. The maximum is six. If you have more than six, consolidate. If you have fewer than four, verify the composition has enough tonal range to work at reproduction sizes.

**3. The composition reads at thumbnail scale.**  
Reduce the motif to 5 × 7 cm on screen. The dominant element, the horizon, and the text must all be immediately legible at this size. If anything is unclear, it will not survive print reduction.

**4. There are no gradients outside the sky plane.**  
Check every colour field: land, architecture, water, foreground. Zero gradient fills permitted in these areas. Sky only.

**5. The text is part of the composition, not applied to it.**  
Remove the location name from the composition and check whether the remaining image feels incomplete — as if something was removed, not merely hidden. If it feels fine without it, the text was not compositionally integrated. Start over on the text placement.

**6. The colour palette is consistent with the brand system.**  
The core brand colours (Charcoal, Paper, Sage, Loess, Stone, Ochre) are the palette library. Location-specific accent colours are permitted if they are derived from the WPA-era palette convention: natural, muted, pigment-adjacent. No saturated digital colours. No neon. No pure primary RGB.

**7. The motif has no period-specific anachronisms.**  
The WPA aesthetic is timeless in its geometry — but it is dated when applied carelessly. Avoid: 1930s dress or transport; vintage letterpress textures as ornament; faux distress or deliberate ageing effects. The prints are new objects, made now, in a style informed by that era. They are not reproductions.

**8. The motif can be printed as a 50 × 70 cm fine art giclée and hold.**  
Zoom to 100% at A2 resolution (≥300 dpi at print size). Check: are edges clean and intentional? Are there any aliased or rasterised artefacts from vector export? Does the composition still feel resolved at this scale, or does the emptiness of large colour fields become uncomfortable? If the latter: add no more detail — instead, reconsider whether the colour choice is strong enough to hold the space.

## 6. First Collection — Applied Direction

The first 3–5 motifs are expected to include subjects from the area around Hirschhorn, Dilsberg, and the Minneburg. This section records directional notes specific to those subjects.

**Hirschhorn:** The view from above — the Burg Hirschhorn on the promontory above the river bend — is the canonical view. Dominant element: the castle keep silhouette against the sky. Supporting element: the town's roofline below. Background field: sky above, river below (as a secondary horizontal band).

**Dilsberg:** The circular hilltop village on its isolated plateau above the valley is the defining image. The roundness of the plateau's edge, seen from the valley road, is both unusual and immediately recognisable. Dominant element: the silhouetted village ring on the hill. Supporting elements: valley floor and river below, sky above.

**Minneburg:** Pure silhouette subject. The ruin against the sky, approached from the valley, with the forest ridge as a lower horizon. The absence of inhabitants and the fragmentary nature of the ruin are assets — this is about geological and historical time, not a day trip.
